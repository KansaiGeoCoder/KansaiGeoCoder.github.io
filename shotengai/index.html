Shotengai: mapping everyday proximity in Kansai

Shotengai — Japan’s covered shopping streets — form walkable corridors of daily life. This study documents their spatial structure in Kobe, Osaka, and Kyoto, and relates morphology to accessibility and retail vitality using open data and reproducible methods.

On this page

1. What is a Shotengai? 2. Why proximity matters 3. Study area & data 4. Methods 5. Key findings 6. Case studies 7. Maps & downloads 8. Contact
Reading time ~8–10 min

1. What is a Shotengai?

Shotengai are pedestrian‑oriented shopping streets, often covered, that serve as local high streets in Japanese cities. They stitch together daily amenities — grocers, clinics, services — within a short walk of homes and transit. Their physical form concentrates doors and signage along continuous frontage, creating a vivid street threshold.

2. Why proximity matters

“Five minutes” describes a practical radius for daily life: roughly 400 meters at a comfortable pace. Mapping the overlap between Shotengai corridors and 5/10/15‑minute walks reveals which neighborhoods enjoy convenient access to everyday retail — and which do not.

Takeaway: Proximity is not only distance; continuity and frontage density shape the real experience of access.

3. Study area & data

This study focuses on the Kansai region — Kobe, Osaka, and Kyoto. Data sources include OpenStreetMap for network and building footprints, municipal open data, and field surveys for frontage use and vacancy.

4. Methods

Analyses are reproducible using QGIS and PostGIS. Key steps include:

  • Network prep: Clean pedestrian network; enforce walkable edges.
  • Isochrones: 5/10/15‑minute travel sheds from Shotengai access points (pgRouting or OSRM).
  • Frontage survey: Capture use (retail/service/residential), vacancy, and openings per 50m.
  • Length & continuity: Measure contiguous covered segments; note breaks/gaps.
  • Accessibility overlay: Intersect isochrones with residential blocks and daily POIs.

5. Key findings

Early results suggest that continuity of covered frontage and the density of small‑format uses correlate with stronger overlapping 10‑minute catchments. Gaps and abrupt terminations often coincide with lower footfall proxies and higher vacancy.

  • ~n km of covered corridors across the three cities.
  • Median Shotengai length: n m (IQR n–n m).
  • Vacancy hotspots align with network discontinuities.
